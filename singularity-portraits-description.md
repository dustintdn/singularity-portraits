# Singularity Portraits

*A live installation on identity, visibility, and light*

## Concept

A camera watches a room. For every face it finds, it doesn’t ask “who is this” in the way a security system would — it doesn’t need a name, a database entry, or a match against a watchlist. Instead, it reads the underlying geometry of the face itself: the particular arrangement of features that makes one face structurally different from every other face on earth. That geometry becomes a seed, and the seed generates a singularity — a small, self-contained ball of light that belongs to that face and no other.

Each singularity is a visual fingerprint rendered as behavior. Its palette (two to three colors), its silhouette (smooth and curved, or sharp and angular), and its motion (drifting, pulsing, orbiting, jittering) all emerge from the same underlying identity signature. One person might summon a slow, curling orange-yellow form. Another might generate something colder, more angular, blue-green and erratic. The mapping isn’t art-directed by hand — it’s generated from the face, so the range of possible singularities is as varied as the range of human faces.

Critically, the relationship is stable: the same face produces the same singularity every time, in this session and the next. The system isn’t improvising a new light show per frame — it’s *recognizing* something about you, persistently, and giving that recognition a visible form. There’s no human-readable “result” on screen (no name tag, no percentage match, no bounding box with a label) — just a glowing, moving object that is unmistakably, consistently *yours* the moment you step into frame, and dissolves the moment you leave it.

## The Underlying Tension

This piece sits deliberately close to the experience of being watched. The technology underneath — facial detection, identity embeddings, persistent recognition — is the same category of technology used in surveillance infrastructure, security systems, and ad-targeting. Singularity Portraits doesn’t hide that kinship; it makes it visible and strange. Instead of identity being extracted for verification or profit, it’s converted into something gratuitous: a small, beautiful, useless object that exists only to express that you are structurally, geometrically *you*, and to do so in front of everyone else in the room.

There is something both delightful and faintly uncomfortable in that. You didn’t consent to a particular color scheme — your face simply *has* one, the way it has a particular nose. The piece offers a soft, aestheticized version of being seen and known by a machine, and lets the viewer sit with whatever mix of wonder and unease that produces. Watching a stranger’s singularity float across the room is watching a piece of their biometric identity rendered in public, stripped of name or context, reduced to (or maybe elevated to) pure light.

## Experience Arc

**Phase one — single subject.** One person, one camera, one singularity. The simplest possible version of the idea: a face in, a unique light-form out, identity made visible and singular. This is the proof of concept — does the relationship between “this is structurally your face” and “this is structurally your light” feel inevitable and alive, or arbitrary and decorative? Everything else depends on getting this right first.

**Phase two — the room.** Multiple faces, multiple cameras (or one wide-angle/overhead camera), multiple singularities simultaneously on screen. As people walk through the space, their singularities track them — drifting across the frame, occasionally drawing near each other, populating the room with a small constellation of personal energy-signatures. A crowd stops being a crowd and becomes a field of individually-rendered identities, each one persistent, each one exposed, each one beautiful.

## Open Questions Worth Sitting With

- Does the piece say anything about *consent* — should there be a visible boundary (a marked zone, a sign) where you know you’re “becoming” a singularity, or is the lack of an opt-in part of the point?
- Should singularities ever interact with each other (merge, repel, resonate) when two people stand close, hinting at relationships between identities — or should each remain strictly solitary, reinforcing the individuality theme?
- Is there a moment of reveal where someone learns *how* their particular colors/shape were derived from their face — demystifying the system — or does the piece work better as a black box, where the mechanism stays felt rather than explained?
